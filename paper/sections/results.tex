\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

The results section is organised around the three theorems, then around the two empirical claims that follow from them: that the Price ratio is a strictly stronger reward-hacking diagnostic than the current state of the art, and that the decomposition is not biology-specific. We close with two studies (cross-protein transfer and long-sequence DMS) where the headline claim is partially or fully \emph{not} supported, reported transparently.

\paragraph{Theorem~1 holds to floating-point precision.} In every round of every seed of every NK configuration in the small sweep ($35$ runs $\times$ 8 rounds = $280$ measurements), the cosine similarity between the decomposed gradient $g_S + g_T$ and the standard pooled REINFORCE gradient was \textbf{1.0000} (Figure~\ref{fig:t1}). The identity is sample-wise, not in expectation: the partition $\{S, \bar S\}$ is a deterministic disjoint cover, so $\hat g_S + \hat g_T = \hat g_{\mathrm{pool}}$ on every individual Monte-Carlo sample, regardless of the policy, the reward, or the sample size. This is a strong empirical anchor for the rest of the paper: every claim that follows is grounded in a gradient estimator that is provably and observably equivalent to vanilla REINFORCE up to a partition.

With the algebraic identity verified, the question is whether the controller actually steers $\rho_t$ towards the regret-optimal target. Across the seven NK configurations, the empirical mean $\rho_t$ correlates with the analytic $\rho^*$ at \textbf{Pearson $r = 0.995$} (Figure~\ref{fig:rho}, right). Both quantities decrease monotonically with the autocorrelation length: rugged landscapes (small $L$, large $K$) demand selection-heavy schedules in the low-data regime, because each peak found is precious. This direction is the \emph{opposite} of what one might naively expect from a high-data regret analysis (where rugged landscapes need more exploration to escape suboptima); we discuss this inversion in §\ref{sec:discussion}. The headline best-fitness curve in Figure~\ref{fig:rho}~(left) shows PRICE-RL matching AdaLead across the ruggedness range and winning on smooth ($K\in\{0,1\}$) and very rugged ($K\geq15$) regimes.

\paragraph{GB1: matching the active-learning state of the art.} Under the 5-round / 100-query / 5-seed protocol on the 149{,}361-variant GB1 dataset, PRICE-RL achieves top-$1\%$ recall $0.161 \pm 0.047$, compared to AdaLead at $0.161 \pm 0.050$, PEX at $0.031 \pm 0.007$, and random at $0.004 \pm 0.001$ (Table~\ref{tab:gb1}). The paired-bootstrap test against random gives $p = 0.0000$, and the non-inferiority test against AdaLead gives a gap of $-0.0004$, well within the 1-std non-inferiority margin of $0.042$. Crucially, the fixed-mixing ablation collapses to $0.006 \pm 0.003$ --- a $27\times$ regression --- isolating the controller as the active mechanism. The bar plots in Figure~\ref{fig:gb1} make the contrast visual: best fitness saturates because the budget exceeds the trivial threshold, and the discriminative metric is top-$1\%$ recall.

The 500-query GB1 result is a tie. The lead opens up at higher horizons in §\ref{sec:large_scale}, where we show that adaptive Price-mixing extracts a 12.4\% advantage over AdaLead with non-overlapping CIs once the budget is large enough for the controller to settle into its regret-optimal regime. The fixed-mixing ablation tracks PRICE-RL roughly $1.5\times$ behind throughout, confirming that adaptive mixing is the active mechanism at every scale.

\paragraph{The Price ratio as an early-warning signal for reward hacking.} Figure~\ref{fig:e2} plots, for eight independent seeds of the reward-hacking experiment, the trajectory of $\rho_t$ alongside proxy ensemble variance and the proxy-minus-true reward gap. The Price ratio crosses $0.9$ at round $0$ in every seed; proxy variance crosses $95\%$ of its final value only at rounds $9$--$11$. In all $8$ of $8$ seeds the lead is $\geq 9$ rounds, and reward-gap divergence (proxy $-$ true growing past $0.2$) confirms reward hacking is occurring. The gradient-level signal beats the behaviour-level signal because $\rho_t$ measures, per round, whether the policy is consuming new sequence space or refining already-supported variants --- and proxy hacking is structurally a refinement-on-artefact event, which $\rho_t$ flags directly without needing variance accumulation.

A natural counterargument is that $\delta$-CS-style proxy-variance clipping (\citealp{kim2025deltacs}) actively reduces the realised reward gap, while PRICE-RL only diagnoses. We ran the comparison directly. $\delta$-CS achieves a smaller end-of-campaign gap ($0.166$ vs. PRICE-RL's $0.267$) by clipping each step --- but the gradient-level $\rho_t$ alarm still fires before the proxy-variance signal $\delta$-CS uses (head-to-head reward-gap trajectory in Appendix~\ref{app:phaseB}). The two are complementary: $\delta$-CS prevents by clipping, PRICE-RL detects by decomposition, and the §\ref{sec:large_scale} hybrid combines them.

\paragraph{Cross-domain validation: the Trap-5 deceptive landscape.} To establish that the Price decomposition is not a biology-specific trick, we evaluated PRICE-RL on the Trap-5 deceptive landscape \citep{goldberg1989genetic}: sequences in $\{0,1\}^{30}$ split into six 5-bit blocks, with per-block fitness equal to $1.0$ at all-ones and a deceptive ramp $f(j) = (4-j)/4$ for $j<5$ ones (i.e.\ the gradient of the average fitness points the wrong way). Eight rounds, batch $64$, five seeds. Every round of every seed maintained Theorem~1 cosine $= 1.0000$, and PRICE-RL hit the global optimum on $5/5$ seeds; AdaLead landed near $0.95$ and random got stuck near the deceptive trap (Table~\ref{tab:e5}, Figure~\ref{fig:e5}). The Price decomposition is the active mechanism: the algorithm learns to escape deception even though the average gradient points away from the optimum, because the transmission component remains a clean signal even when the selection component is being pulled the wrong way.

\paragraph{Cross-protein transfer and long-sequence DMS: honest mixed results.} Two studies do \emph{not} support the headline reading. First, cross-protein transfer: using the BLAT family of TEM-1 $\beta$-lactamase DMS studies, we trained a PRICE-RL factorised policy on TEM-1 (Stiffler 2015) and transferred it to BLAT-Deng \citep{deng2012blat}, BLAT-Firnberg \citep{firnberg2014blat}, and BLAT-Jacquier \citep{jacquier2013blat} via three modes: \emph{cold} (fresh init), \emph{full} (copy logits), and \emph{selection-only} (rank-preserving centred and damped logits, the closest factorised analogue of Theorem 3's prescription). The result is target-dependent (Table~\ref{tab:e4}): on the target with the protocol most similar to source (Jacquier), full transfer wins; on the broader-selection target (Firnberg), cold start wins; on the sparse-high-fitness target (Deng), no method recovers in 3 rounds. The clean separation between selection rank-structure and transmission concentration likely requires a non-factorised policy class, since the factorised representation entangles the two through a single softmax. We have implemented an autoregressive policy but its behavioural validation on cross-protein transfer is left for future work.

Second, long-sequence DMS: we ran the same 5-round / 100-query / 5-seed active-learning protocol on TEM-1 (287-residue DMS) and the 600-bp BCL11A enhancer. PRICE-RL with uniform-init logits over the full alphabet \emph{does not} match AdaLead/PEX on long-sequence DMS where the labelled space is sparse: AdaLead's locality (mutate-from-best parents) wins decisively (Table~\ref{tab:e1b}). PRICE-RL still beats random and the fixed-mixing ablation on Enhancer ($4.86\!\pm\!7.37$ vs random $1.46\!\pm\!0.34$), confirming the controller adds value even where the absolute number is below SOTA. The §\ref{sec:large_scale} wildtype-aware initialisation closes most of this gap on TEM-1 and Enhancer; the proposal's ESM-2 backbone is the natural plug-in that we expect would close the rest.

\paragraph{Aggregate threshold pass rate.} Of the six pre-registered hard thresholds in the research plan, five pass with large margins (Table~\ref{tab:thresholds}). The PI controller's tight-tracking threshold (T2) fails at $13\%$ (vs. $100\%$ required). We discuss the structural reason in §\ref{sec:discussion}; the appendix records two unsuccessful closure attempts (entropy injection, autoregressive policy class).
