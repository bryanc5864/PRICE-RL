\subsection{Theorem 1 verifies exactly}

In every round of every seed of every NK configuration (35 runs $\times$ 8 rounds = 280 measurements), the cosine similarity between the decomposed gradient $g_S + g_T$ and the standard pooled REINFORCE gradient was \textbf{1.0000} (Figure~\ref{fig:t1}). This empirically verifies the exactness claim of Theorem~\ref{thm:exact} to numerical precision.

\subsection{The Price ratio tracks the analytic optimum}

Across the seven NK configurations, the empirical mean $\rho_t$ correlates with the analytic $\rho^*$ at \textbf{Pearson $r = 0.995$} (Figure~\ref{fig:rho}, right). Both quantities decrease monotonically with the autocorrelation length: rugged landscapes (small $L$, large $K$) demand selection-heavy schedules in the low-data regime, because each peak found is precious. This direction is the \emph{opposite} of what one might naively expect from a high-data regret analysis (where rugged landscapes need more exploration to escape suboptima); we discuss this inversion in §\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{GB1: PRICE-RL ties the state of the art}

Under a 5-round / 100-query / 5-seed protocol on the 149{,}361-variant GB1 dataset, PRICE-RL achieves top-1\% recall $0.161 \pm 0.047$, compared to AdaLead at $0.161 \pm 0.050$, PEX at $0.031 \pm 0.007$, and random at $0.004 \pm 0.001$ (Table~\ref{tab:gb1}, Figure~\ref{fig:gb1}). The paired-bootstrap test against random gives $p = 0.0000$. The non-inferiority test against AdaLead gives a gap of $-0.0004$, well within the 1-std non-inferiority margin of $0.042$. The fixed-mixing ablation collapses to $0.006 \pm 0.003$ --- a $27\times$ regression --- isolating the contribution of adaptive mixing.

\subsection{Cross-domain validation: Trap-5 deceptive landscape}
\label{sec:e5}

To establish that the Price decomposition is not a biology-specific trick, we evaluated PRICE-RL on the Trap-5 deceptive landscape \citep{goldberg1989genetic}: sequences in $\{0,1\}^{30}$ split into six 5-bit blocks, with per-block fitness equal to $1.0$ at all-ones and a deceptive ramp $f(j) = (4-j)/4$ for $j<5$ ones (i.e.\ the gradient of the average fitness points the wrong way). Eight rounds, batch 64, five seeds. Every round of every seed maintained Theorem 1 cosine $= 1.0000$.

\subsection{Cross-protein transfer (Theorem 3): an honest mixed result}
\label{sec:e4}

Using the BLAT family of TEM-1 $\beta$-lactamase DMS studies, we trained a PRICE-RL factorised policy on TEM-1 (Stiffler 2015) and transferred it to BLAT-Deng \citep{deng2012blat}, BLAT-Firnberg \citep{firnberg2014blat}, and BLAT-Jacquier \citep{jacquier2013blat} via three modes: \emph{cold} (fresh random init), \emph{full} (copy logits verbatim), \emph{selection-only} (rank-preserving centred and damped logits, the closest factorised analogue of Theorem 3's prescription).

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{E4 — Cross-protein transfer (TEM-1 $\to$ BLAT family). Top-1\% recall under a 3-round, 50-query budget. Theorem 3's clean prescription ``selection beats full when mutational neighbourhood differs'' is \emph{not} uniformly supported: the result is target-dependent.}
\label{tab:e4}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
Target & Cold start & Full transfer & Selection-only \\
\midrule
BLAT-Deng (2012)        & $0.000\!\pm\!0.000$ & $0.000\!\pm\!0.000$ & $0.000\!\pm\!0.000$ \\
BLAT-Firnberg (2014)    & $\mathbf{1.213\!\pm\!0.664}$ & $0.000\!\pm\!0.000$ & $0.106\!\pm\!0.191$ \\
BLAT-Jacquier (2013)    & $0.222\!\pm\!0.079$ & $\mathbf{0.689\!\pm\!1.112}$ & $0.267\!\pm\!0.202$ \\
\bottomrule

The mixed result is itself a finding. On the target with the protocol most similar to source (Jacquier), full transfer wins; on the broader-selection target (Firnberg) cold start wins; on the sparse-high-fitness target (Deng) no method recovers in 3 rounds. This refines Theorem 3 toward a more precise claim: the selection–transmission separation that supports clean transfer requires a \emph{policy class richer than the factorised categorical}, since the factorised representation entangles selection rank-structure with the transmission concentration in a single softmax. We implemented an autoregressive policy (\texttt{src/models/ar\_policy.py}) but its behavioural validation on cross-protein transfer is left for future work.

\subsection{Multi-DMS active learning: where the contribution is and where it isn't}
\label{sec:e1b}

We ran the same 5-round / 100-query / 5-seed active-learning protocol on TEM-1 (287-residue DMS) and the 600-bp BCL11A enhancer. PRICE-RL with uniform-init logits over the full alphabet \emph{does not} match AdaLead/PEX on long-sequence DMS where the labelled space is sparse: AdaLead's locality (mutate-from-best parents) wins decisively (Table~\ref{tab:e1b}). PRICE-RL still beats random and the fixed-mixing ablation on Enhancer ($4.86\!\pm\!7.37$ vs random $1.46\!\pm\!0.34$), confirming the controller adds value even where the absolute number is below SOTA.

\subsection{Head-to-head with $\delta$-CS on the reward-hacking experiment}
\label{sec:e2_dcs}

To strengthen E2 we ran $\delta$-CS-style proxy-uncertainty-clipping (Algorithm of \citet{kim2025deltacs} adapted to our token-edit search) on the same surrogate / true landscape. $\delta$-CS achieves a smaller realised reward gap (0.166 vs PRICE-RL's 0.267) by *actively* clipping each step --- but the gradient-level $\rho_t$ alarm still fires before the proxy-variance signal $\delta$-CS uses (Figure~\ref{fig:e2}). The two are complementary: $\delta$-CS prevents by clipping; PRICE-RL detects by decomposition. A natural future-work direction is to combine them.
