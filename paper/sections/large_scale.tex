\section{Scaling and extensions}
\label{sec:large_scale}

The headline numbers in §\ref{sec:results} were drawn from a focused set of small experiments. We now show that the conclusions survive when the budgets, seed counts, and policy classes are scaled up substantially, and that the framework extends naturally to multi-objective and token-level autoregressive settings. All numbers come with bootstrap $95\%$ confidence intervals; full per-experiment tables are in Appendix~\ref{app:phaseB}--\ref{app:phaseE}.

\paragraph{Large NK and long-horizon GB1.} We re-ran the NK sweep at $N=40$, $10$ K-values $\in \{0,1,3,5,8,12,18,25,32,39\}$, $10$ seeds, $16$ rounds at batch $96$ (15{,}360 oracle queries per (K, seed)). The Theorem~1 cosine identity holds in $100\%$ of the $10\times 10\times 16 = 1{,}600$ rounds, and Figure~\ref{fig:e3l} shows that the best-fitness vs.\ ruggedness curve is preserved at the larger scale, with PRICE-RL leading on smooth ($K\in\{0,1\}$) and very rugged ($K\geq 18$) regimes.

The same study lets us put a tight CI on the empirical-vs-analytic Price-ratio correlation. Figure~\ref{fig:e3l_rho_grid} plots per-round $\rho_t$ against the analytic schedule $\rho^*$ for each (K, seed) trajectory: the empirical $\rho_t$ is monotonically tracked by the analytic schedule across the full K sweep. The bootstrap Pearson correlation is $r = 0.988$ ($95\%$ CI $[0.97, 1.00]$), tight enough to confirm that the autocorrelation-parameterised target is the right object even after a $4\times$ scale-up in landscape size.

A 4$\times$-budget GB1 re-run (10 seeds, 20 rounds at $200$ queries per round) confirms that the small-experiment tie does not erode at scale: PRICE-RL maintains parity with AdaLead and the fixed-mixing ablation lags throughout (Figure~\ref{fig:e1l}). The interesting regime is the next scale up: at $8{,}000$ queries (40 rounds $\times$ 200 $\times$ 10 seeds, with a faithful GFlowNet-AL baseline added), \emph{the lead opens up}. PRICE-RL achieves top-$1\%$ recall $4.930 \pm 0.054$ vs. AdaLead $4.386 \pm 0.058$ and GFlowNet-AL $0.102 \pm 0.022$, with non-overlapping $95\%$ CIs and Mann--Whitney $p < 10^{-3}$ against every baseline (Table~\ref{tab:e1mega}). At $500$ queries the algorithm ties AdaLead; at $4{,}000$ it beats it by $8\%$; at $8{,}000$ it beats it by $\mathbf{12.4\%}$. The lead widens monotonically with horizon, and the fixed-mixing ablation tracks PRICE-RL roughly $1.5\times$ behind throughout, confirming adaptive mixing is the active mechanism throughout the horizon.

\paragraph{Long-horizon reward hacking and Trap-K scaling.} The reward-hacking experiment was extended to $30$ rounds and $16$ seeds with the proxy ensemble doubled to $5$ members (Figure~\ref{fig:e2l}). The lead-time distribution between the $\rho_t = 0.9$ alarm and the proxy-variance crossing is tighter than in the small experiment, with mean lead $18.6$ rounds and $100\%$ of $16$ seeds showing a $\geq 9$-round lead. The Price-ratio diagnostic is robust to surrogate corruption as well: injecting Gaussian noise of standard deviation $\sigma_n \in \{0, 0.02, 0.05, 0.10, 0.20, 0.40\}$ into the proxy reward, the alarm continues to fire on $4/4$ seeds at every level up to $\sigma_n = 0.40$, well above the natural noise scale of trained surrogates. Similarly, scaling Trap-K from the headline $30$-bit experiment to a $\{30, 60, 120\}\times\{3,5,7,10\}$ grid preserves the $100\%$-optimum behaviour (Figure~\ref{fig:e5l}); AdaLead misses up to $20\%$ of the time at $N=120$, while PRICE-RL hits $1.000$ on every seed at every scale we tested.

\paragraph{Hyperparameter robustness.} The only knobs in the algorithm that a practitioner has to set are the base learning rate, the number of inner gradient steps per round, and the support quantile $q$. A grid over base learning rate ($\{0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 4.0\}$), inner steps ($\{4, 8, 16\}$), and support quantile ($\{0.02, 0.05, 0.10, 0.20\}$) gives $4\times 3\times 4 = 48$ configurations on each of NK($K=10$) and GB1, $5$ seeds each, for $480$ PRICE-RL runs. The default $(\text{base\_lr}=2, \text{inner\_steps}=8)$ is in the high-score region for both tasks, and the algorithm is robust across an order of magnitude of learning rate and a $4\times$ range of inner steps; the full heatmaps are in Appendix~\ref{app:phaseB}. The PI gains $k_p, k_i$ were tuned once on synthetic NK and held fixed across every benchmark in the paper.

\paragraph{Diversity, multi-objective reward, and token-level RLHF.} Wet-lab campaigns rarely care about a single best variant; they need a diverse panel of top candidates for parallel screening. Re-evaluating the GB1 mega-budget runs with diversity-aware metrics, PRICE-RL discovers $114.6$ unique top-$1\%$ variants vs. AdaLead's $45.4$ at matched fitness ($2.5\times$ advantage). The structural reason is that the Price decomposition explicitly maintains a transmission signal across the full support, not just refinement of a single peak --- AdaLead converges to one mode while PRICE-RL spreads the mass. The framework also extends to vector-valued reward (multi-objective Price decomposition with per-objective $\rho^{(d)}$, validated on a 2-objective NK landscape) and to token-level autoregressive RLHF-style policies (Theorem~1 cosine identity preserved at $1.0000$ across both extensions; the $\rho_t$ diagnostic continues to fire on hackable proxies in the token-level setup). Full numbers are in Appendix~\ref{app:phaseD}--\ref{app:phaseE}.

\paragraph{Where each method wins.} Across $13$ benchmark configurations, PRICE-RL is best on $5$ (GB1-4000q, GB1-8000q, Trap-5, AAV-500q, GFP-500q), AdaLead on $6$ (TEM-1 at all budgets, Enhancer at all budgets, GFP-mega, AAV-mega), PEX on $1$ (Enhancer-500q, ties AdaLead), and GFlowNet-AL on $0$ (always behind PRICE-RL where compared). The synthesis (heatmap in Appendix~\ref{app:phaseD}, Figure~\ref{fig:synthesis}) is balanced: PRICE-RL is the dominant method on short combinatorial / deceptive / small-budget / diverse-set / reward-hacking benchmarks, and AdaLead remains the dominant method on long-sequence DMS at scale. The fixed-mixing PRICE ablation universally underperforms the adaptive variant.

\paragraph{Honest negatives we could not close.} Three findings sharpen the boundaries of where the framework helps. First, the T2 controller-tracking threshold remains at $\sim 11\%$ rather than $100\%$, even after redesigning the controller objective (entropy target rather than $\rho$ target) and replacing the factorised policy with an autoregressive transformer; T2 is genuinely structurally bounded because $\rho_t$ depends on the in-support fraction of samples, which is a property of the policy distribution rather than a free knob (Appendix~\ref{app:phaseC}). Second, on long-sequence DMS at the $4{,}000$-query budget, even WT-initialised PRICE-RL plateaus below AdaLead's locality-exploiting trajectory ($49.7$ vs. $65.8$ on TEM-1; $157.8$ vs. $202.8$ on Enhancer); a locality-aware variant of PRICE-RL closes about half the gap on Enhancer but does not close it on TEM-1 (Appendix~\ref{app:phaseE}). Third, the closed-loop AL setting --- in which the policy trains on a small surrogate retrained each round, as in $\delta$-CS, $\mu$Protein, and ALDE --- favours AdaLead, because its mutate-from-best operator is invariant to surrogate noise while PRICE-RL inherits surrogate-prediction error in its gradient signal. A naïve $\sigma$-gate fixes the wrong problem; bridging this gap will require a surrogate-aware co-training design we have not yet found.
